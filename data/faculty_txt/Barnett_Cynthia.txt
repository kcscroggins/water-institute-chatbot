Research Focus:
Cynthia Barnett is one of the most prominent environmental journalists in the United States, known especially for her work on water, climate, and environmental justice. She sits at the intersection of investigative journalism, environmental humanities, public-facing science communication, and water policy. Her narrative nonfiction has shaped public understanding of water scarcity, climate risk, and environmental justice. At UF, she teaches environmental journalism, mentors investigative reporters, and works closely with scientists to translate research into public impact. She served as faculty team member on UF Water Institute's Water Institute Graduate Fellows (WIGF) cohort alongside water-science faculty. She also serves as faculty advisor/editor for UF student investigative water reporting, including WUFT's statewide water-quality project "Watershed" funded by the Pulitzer Center.

Books:
- "Rain: A Natural and Cultural History" (Crown/Random House, 2015) - Widely acclaimed by NYT, NPR; treats rain as climate system, culture, and power
- "Blue Revolution: Unmaking America's Water Crisis" (Beacon Press, 2011) - Deep dive into U.S. water infrastructure failures, policy, politics, inequality, and ecology
- "Mirage: Florida and the Vanishing Water of the Eastern U.S." (University of Michigan Press, 2007) - Florida as case study for overdevelopment, aquifer depletion, and climate vulnerability
- "The Sound of the Sea" (W.W. Norton, 2016) - Shortlisted for major science writing awards
